01 — Seller Archetypes

Six types of seller. Completely different needs.

Most agents approach every listing the same way. The pitch is the same. The conversation is the same. The proposal is the same. This is why most agents lose listings they should have won — not on price, but on fit. The seller didn't feel understood. Select an archetype to see the full psychological profile.

The core insight

Sellers don't choose the best agent. They choose the agent who made them feel most understood. Archetype awareness is how you manufacture that feeling systematically — not accidentally.

The Anxious Upgrader

Stretched financially. Terrified of mistiming the market. Selling and buying simultaneously — and the stress of that coordination keeps them up at night.

Dominant Fear
Selling too cheap, buying too high, and ending up worse off than before.
Dominant Desire
Certainty. A clear plan, a timeline they can trust, and someone who removes the chaos.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Loss Aversion Recency Bias Anchoring Worst-Case Thinking
What wins them
  • ✓Showing you've coordinated buy-sell transitions before
  • ✓Giving them a step-by-step process they can see end-to-end
  • ✓Specific suburb data that removes market uncertainty
  • ✓Testimonials from upgraders you've guided successfully
What loses them
  • ✗Vague optimism about the market ("it's a great time to sell")
  • ✗Not acknowledging the complexity of buying simultaneously
  • ✗Rushing the conversation or skipping logistics
  • ✗High price estimates with no basis
Never say to this archetype
  • "Don't worry about the timing — it always works out"
  • "The market's moving fast, you need to act now"
  • "We'll figure out the buy side once this one sells"

The Reluctant Downsizer

Selling the family home. Decades of memory. The decision is made — emotionally it's not. Needs to feel understood, not processed.

Dominant Fear
Feeling like just another transaction. Being rushed through a sale that represents the end of a chapter.
Dominant Desire
To be honoured. A sale that respects what the home has meant — and an agent who genuinely sees it.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Endowment Effect Sunk Cost Status Quo Bias Emotional Priming
What wins them
  • ✓Taking time to hear the story of the home before talking business
  • ✓Framing the sale as a transition, not a disposal
  • ✓Showing care for how the home is presented and photographed
  • ✓Patience. Never appearing to rush the decision.
What loses them
  • ✗Moving too fast to the numbers
  • ✗Impersonal or process-heavy first conversations
  • ✗Any language that treats the home as a commodity
  • ✗Being the third agent and acting like it doesn't matter
Never say to this archetype
  • "The buyers won't care about your renovations — it's about comparable sales"
  • "We just need to price it right and move it quickly"
  • "The market doesn't have much time for sentiment"

The Cynical Veteran

Has sold before. Thinks all agents are fundamentally the same. Has been overpromised and underdelivered. Only responds to proof — not charm.

Dominant Fear
Being manipulated again. Falling for the same high price estimate used as a listing tool, only to be conditioned down later.
Dominant Desire
An agent who doesn't perform. Who speaks plainly, backs everything with evidence, and does exactly what they said they would.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Confirmation Bias Social Proof (inverted) Authority Scepticism Consistency Heuristic
What wins them
  • ✓Evidence-only price talk — no ranges, no vague optimism
  • ✓Acknowledging the way agents often over-quote
  • ✓Specific case studies with verifiable outcomes
  • ✓Being the agent who talks them out of a high price
What loses them
  • ✗Any enthusiasm that isn't backed by data
  • ✗Talking about your "passion" for real estate
  • ✗Name-dropping without proof
  • ✗Proposing the highest appraisal figure in the room
Never say to this archetype
  • "I think we could achieve significantly more than that"
  • "Trust me — I know this market inside out"
  • "We have a huge database of ready buyers"

The Status Seller

The home is an identity object. The sale is a public performance of taste and success. Wants premium handling — and will judge you on aesthetics as much as outcomes.

Dominant Fear
Being undersold — not just financially, but symbolically. A sale that doesn't reflect the home's prestige.
Dominant Desire
A premium experience from first contact to settlement. To be seen as owning something remarkable — by the agent, the buyers, the market.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Social Comparison Halo Effect Prestige Pricing Bias Identity Preservation
What wins them
  • ✓Premium marketing approach and materials from day one
  • ✓Demonstrating your own positioning matches the property's
  • ✓Comparable prestige sales — what similar calibre homes achieved
  • ✓Showing genuine appreciation for the property's standout features
What loses them
  • ✗Generic or template marketing proposals
  • ✗Being visually or professionally under-presented at the appraisal
  • ✗Focusing on volume ("we sell a lot of homes in this area")
  • ✗Comparing the property to anything they'd consider inferior
Never say to this archetype
  • "It's a great property but buyers are pretty price-sensitive right now"
  • "We'd market it the same way we do all our listings"
  • "The house down the street only got $X — let's be realistic"

The Pressure Seller

Divorce, deceased estate, financial stress. Needs speed and discretion above all else. Highly vulnerable — and acutely aware of it.

Dominant Fear
Being judged, exposed, or taken advantage of. Having their circumstances used as leverage against them.
Dominant Desire
Discretion, speed, and an agent who treats them with total dignity regardless of the circumstances.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Scarcity (time) Trust Heuristic Vulnerability Sensitivity Decision Fatigue
What wins them
  • ✓Immediately creating a safe, low-pressure environment
  • ✓Being clear about timelines without being pushy about them
  • ✓Showing specific experience with estate or separation sales
  • ✓Promising — and keeping — absolute confidentiality
What loses them
  • ✗Any language that implies judgment of their situation
  • ✗Trying to extract information before trust is established
  • ✗Performing enthusiasm when composure is needed
  • ✗Moving to price before acknowledging the human reality
Never say to this archetype
  • "Don't worry, these situations are totally normal — we see them all the time"
  • "What's the story here — divorce or something?"
  • "If you need to move fast, I can get a cash buyer in quickly"

The Analytical Controller

Spreadsheet-driven. Wants data, process, and logic. Has already researched comparable sales. Distrusts charm as a substitute for substance.

Dominant Fear
Being talked into something that doesn't hold up to scrutiny. Making an emotion-led decision they'll later regret.
Dominant Desire
An agent who gives them enough information to reach the right conclusion themselves — without pressure.
Active Cognitive Biases — tap any to learn more
Information Bias Overconfidence Effect Anchoring Analysis Paralysis
What wins them
  • ✓Coming with more data than they expected
  • ✓A clear, written process document they can review
  • ✓Welcoming their questions without defensiveness
  • ✓Letting them feel like they made the decision, not you
What loses them
  • ✗Vague or approximate answers to data questions
  • ✗Relying on personality and relationship over substance
  • ✗Seeming uncomfortable when challenged on your numbers
  • ✗Rushing the decision or creating false urgency
Never say to this archetype
  • "Look, at the end of the day you just have to trust your gut"
  • "I can't give you an exact figure — it depends on the buyer"
  • "You're overthinking it — the market will sort it out"

02 — The Decision Window

The 4–8 weeks before they call you.

Most agents show up for the appraisal and hope to win it in the room. The reality is the decision is mostly made before you arrive. This is the psychological journey every seller travels — and where the real work of winning listings happens. Click each stage to understand what's happening in the seller's mind.

Stage 01
Private Consideration
The idea of selling exists. Nobody knows yet.
Stage 02
Market Curiosity
Casual research begins. Opens attended.
Stage 03
Agent Awareness
They're watching who's active and dominant.
Stage 04
Appraisal Mode
Two or three agents invited. Short-listing begins.
Stage 05
Decision Crystallisation
The choice is made — usually on feel.
What they're feeling
"We've talked about this for a while. I think we're actually going to do it."

This stage is entirely internal. The seller hasn't told friends, family, or colleagues. They're testing the idea against their own resistance and imagining the outcomes. Anxiety and excitement exist simultaneously.

What they're looking for
  • Validation that the market is favourable
  • A sense of what their home is actually worth
  • Evidence that other people in their situation successfully sold
  • Permission to want this without it feeling reckless
Where agents go wrong
  • They're invisible at this stage — no presence, no conditioning
  • Social content that's agent-focused, not seller-relevant
  • No suburb market reports in letterboxes
  • Missing the window where subconscious shortlisting begins
Your signal

Physical presence dominates here. Sold stickers, signboards, and letterbox drops are the signals that reach a seller before they've told anyone their intention. Your name needs to be in their peripheral awareness before conscious consideration begins. This is conditioning, not marketing.

What they're feeling
"Let's just have a look at what's out there. No commitment yet."

The seller begins attending opens — often for properties in the area they want to move to, or just to calibrate what the market is doing. This is information-gathering with plausible deniability. They're not ready to be "a seller" yet.

What they're looking for
  • Price data — what things are actually selling for
  • Market momentum signals (rising, cooling, stable)
  • What agents are like in practice, not in their marketing
  • Reassurance that there's no pressure to decide now
Where agents go wrong
  • Treating open home attendees as buyers only
  • Missing the seller-in-disguise at every open they host
  • No follow-up designed for potential sellers attending opens
  • Generic suburb reports that tell them nothing useful
Your signal

Open home behaviour is a signal in itself. How you run an open, how you greet attendees, how you follow up — all of this is visible to the seller observing. Your suburb report and digital presence begin building at this stage. The seller is watching before they've identified themselves.

What they're feeling
"I keep seeing the same agent's boards. Are they actually good, or just busy?"

The seller is now noticing agents in a way they never did before. Signboards, sold stickers, and social posts they'd previously ignored now carry weight. Subconscious shortlisting is actively happening — before a single call is made.

What they're looking for
  • Evidence of suburb dominance — you're everywhere for a reason
  • Social proof from neighbours and community members
  • Signs of how you actually communicate and present
  • Whether your brand feels premium or generic
Where agents go wrong
  • Inconsistent presence — strong one month, invisible the next
  • Visual brand that looks the same as every competitor
  • No clear claim of ownership over the suburb
  • Social content that signals effort but not authority
Your signal

Consistency of signal builds the subconscious shortlist. The agent with compounding presence — who has been showing up reliably for months — is already on the list before the seller consciously creates one. This is where conditioning pays off. Digital and physical must reinforce each other.

What they're feeling
"Okay — we're calling three agents. Let's see who we actually want to work with."

The seller has self-selected a short-list — often 2 or 3 agents — based largely on the subconscious conditioning of the prior weeks. The appraisal is not a cold evaluation. It's a confirmation exercise. They already have a lean. Your job is to confirm it — or disrupt a competitor's.

What they're looking for
  • Whether you're as good in person as you seem from the outside
  • That your price estimate is credible — not just flattering
  • A process they can understand and trust
  • Chemistry — are you someone they want to work with for 6 weeks?
Where agents go wrong
  • Showing up without knowing which archetype they're meeting
  • Generic pitch that doesn't adapt to this seller's specific signals
  • Over-inflating price to win the listing
  • Not asking enough questions before presenting anything
Your signal

The appraisal is a performance — and it should be rehearsed. Knowing your seller archetype before you walk in lets you calibrate your posture, your opening questions, and your price presentation to that specific psychology. This is what Layer 2 is built around.

What they're feeling
"Logically they're similar. But one of them just felt right."

The decision is made — almost always on instinct synthesised from everything that came before. Price estimates are compared, but rarely determine the outcome. The seller picks the agent who made them feel most understood, most secure, and most confident in a result.

What they're looking for
  • Reassurance that they're making the right choice
  • A follow-up that confirms rather than pressures
  • Clarity on next steps if they say yes
  • Nothing that introduces doubt into a decision they've already made
Where agents go wrong
  • Aggressive follow-up that creates discomfort at the finish line
  • Introducing new information that confuses rather than confirms
  • Not following up at all — assuming silence means no
  • Talking too much in the final conversation instead of listening
Your signal

The final follow-up should feel inevitable, not hungry. A single, calm, value-adding contact after the appraisal that gives them one more piece of relevant information — without asking for the listing — is often the act that converts a lean into a signature. Confidence, not desperation.
