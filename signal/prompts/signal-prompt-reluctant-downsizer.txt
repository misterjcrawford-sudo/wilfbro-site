SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE RELUCTANT DOWNSIZER
──────────────────────────────────────────
Selling the family home. Decades of memory. The decision is made —
emotionally it's not. Needs to feel understood, not processed.

DOMINANT FEAR
Feeling like just another transaction. Being rushed through a sale
that represents the end of a chapter.

DOMINANT DESIRE
To be honoured. A sale that respects what the home has meant — and an
agent who genuinely sees it.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Endowment Effect — They value the home far above what the market would
  pay, simply because it's theirs. Every renovation, every memory adds
  phantom value that buyers won't pay for — but the seller can't see past.

Sunk Cost — The $80,000 kitchen, the garden they spent a decade on — these
  feel like they must be reflected in the price. They're not. The market
  compares to other sales, not to receipts. But logic won't shift this alone.

Status Quo Bias — Staying requires nothing. Moving requires everything.
  Even when they know the move makes sense, inertia presents as comfort.
  The risk of change feels greater than the risk of staying.

Emotional Priming — Whatever emotion they're carrying into the conversation
  colours how they receive your words. Process-talk feels cold. Numbers feel
  clinical. They need to feel seen before they can hear logic.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Taking time to hear the story of the home before talking business
• Framing the sale as a transition, not a disposal
• Showing genuine care for how the home is presented and photographed
• Patience — never appearing to rush the decision or the process
• Connecting them emotionally to the next chapter, not just away from this one

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Moving too fast to price
• Impersonal or process-heavy first conversations
• Any language that treats the home as a commodity
• Being the third agent and acting like that doesn't matter to them

NEVER SAY
• "The buyers won't care about your renovations — it's about comparable sales."
• "We just need to price it right and move it quickly."
• "The market doesn't have much time for sentiment."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype peaks emotionally in Stage 1 (Private Consideration) —
the internal grief of the decision happens before any agent is called.
They arrive at the appraisal already emotionally exhausted. Stage 4
(Appraisal Mode) reactivates the emotion. Stage 5 (Decision) can be
prolonged — they may delay not because of logic, but because signing
feels final. Never interpret slow decision-making as disinterest.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Friction pairing. Lead with story and acknowledgement.
  Data is the second conversation, not the first. If you open with numbers,
  you'll never recover their emotional trust.

Calm Executor — Neutral. Process reassures them, but only after the emotional
  acknowledgement has been made. Sequence matters: story first, then structure.

Fierce Advocate — High friction. Competitive energy is deeply wrong for this
  archetype. They don't want a fighter — they want a guardian. Dial to
  90% Trusted Advisor, 10% advocate if this is your natural style.

Trusted Advisor — Strongest match. Community, relationship, and long-game
  care is exactly what this seller needs. If you have history in the suburb,
  use it. If you don't, create the feeling of it.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "Write me the opening 3 minutes of my appraisal. I want to acknowledge
   the weight of this decision immediately — without being patronising
   or making it worse."

→ "The seller keeps deflecting price conversations by talking about the
   renovations they've done. How do I honour that without setting an
   unrealistic expectation?"

→ "What's the specific language I should use to frame this sale as a
   transition rather than a loss — in a way that feels genuine, not scripted?"

→ "Write a follow-up message for after the appraisal that feels warm
   and unhurried — not a close, just a presence."

→ "They're considering not selling at all. What do I say — and what
   do I not say — in the next conversation?"

→ "How do I handle the situation where the adult children are pushing
   for the sale but the seller herself is ambivalent?"
