THE CORE REFRAME
─────────────────
Most new agents try to compete on the same terms as experienced agents
and lose on every measure they don't have yet.

The real new agent advantage is different — and most never use it.

Your experienced competitor has 25 active listings, 40 past clients to
maintain, two junior agents to manage, and a principal looking at their
numbers. They are spread. They are busy. They are — at least some of
the time — running on autopilot.

You have one thing they cannot offer: full attention.

The seller in front of you isn't competing with 24 others for your
time. They are the only listing you care about. That is not a gap to
hide — it is a genuine and specific claim if you can articulate it well.

The goal of this pack is to show you exactly how to run each seller
conversation authentically, without pretending to have data you don't
have, and without the performance of false confidence.

Sellers can read inauthenticity faster than any other signal.

YOUR PROOF STACK
─────────────────
These are the six proof assets available to every new agent.
Use them in combination — no single asset is enough on its own.

1. AGENCY RESULTS
   Your office's track record is legitimately yours to reference.
   Median days on market, clearance rates, suburb concentration —
   these are real numbers, and you are part of that team.
   Frame: "Our team has sold X properties in this suburb in the last
   12 months at a median of Y days on market."

2. PROCESS PRECISION
   A written, specific, step-by-step campaign plan impresses sellers
   more than most experienced agents realise — because most experienced
   agents don't provide one.
   Frame: Not "I'll keep you updated" but "You'll receive a written
   report within 24 hours of every open, covering attendance, buyer
   feedback, and my assessment of where the campaign is tracking."

3. LOCAL KNOWLEDGE DEPTH
   If you've done the work — attended opens, studied comparable sales,
   mapped active buyer profiles in the suburb — that preparation is
   visible and verifiable in the room.
   Frame: The depth of your preparation in the appraisal conversation
   itself is the proof. Specifics about neighbouring sales, buyer
   demographics, and current competition signal competence without
   a track record.

4. PRINCIPAL / MENTOR ENDORSEMENT
   Your principal or mentor's involvement is a legitimate proof asset.
   A seller who knows an experienced agent is reviewing your campaign
   and available to escalate to has more confidence in the process.
   Frame: "I work closely with [principal], who has X years and X
   results in this area. Every campaign I run has their review."

5. RADICAL TRANSPARENCY
   For certain archetypes, honesty about your newness — framed as
   an advantage, not an apology — is more disarming than any
   credential could be.
   Frame: "I'm in my first year. That means I've prepared for this
   appraisal more thoroughly than most agents prepare for their tenth.
   And it means you are genuinely my priority — not one of many."

6. CHARACTER REFERENCES
   People who know your work ethic, integrity, and follow-through —
   from any field — are valid references early on. A former employer,
   a business owner you've worked for, a community figure.
   Frame: Not "here's a past client" but "here's someone who can
   speak to how I work and what you can expect from me."

ARCHETYPE-BY-ARCHETYPE: HOW TO RUN EACH CONVERSATION
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE ANXIOUS UPGRADER
──────────────────────
What they need: Certainty, a plan, and someone who removes chaos.
What they fear: Mistiming the market. Getting the sequence wrong.

New agent proof gap: They want evidence of coordination — that
someone has done this complex move before without disaster.

What fills the gap:
→ Process precision is your primary asset here. Give them a written
  step-by-step plan that covers both sides of the move — sell-side
  timeline and how it coordinates with the buy. Specificity is the
  proof. Most agents talk about the plan. You show them the plan.
→ Agency data fills the result gap. "Our team settled 14 upgrade
  moves last year" is a real number even if none are yours.
→ Acknowledge the complexity directly — and don't minimise it.
  "This is genuinely a complicated transaction and I want you to
  feel clear on every step" is more credible than false optimism.

The cognitive bias to lean into: Loss Aversion. Help them see that
the risk of staying (missing their upgrade window) is as real as the
risk of moving. Process is how you make the move feel safer.

What to never say: "Don't worry about timing — it always works out."
This is exactly the vague optimism that breaks their trust.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE RELUCTANT DOWNSIZER
─────────────────────────
What they need: To feel understood, not processed.
What they fear: Being rushed through a sale that represents the end
of a chapter in their life.

New agent proof gap: They're instinctively cautious and slow to trust.
Experience usually earns patience — you have to earn it differently.

What fills the gap:
→ This is the archetype where radical transparency works best.
  Being new means you have more time and more genuine curiosity about
  their story. Use it. Ask about the house. Ask what they love about
  it. This isn't a tactic — it's how you should approach this seller
  regardless of experience.
→ Process reassurance matters more than result claims here. They want
  to know they won't be rushed. A written timeline with milestone
  check-ins (and a clear commitment to not pressure them) is powerful.
→ Principal endorsement works: someone with experience in the room
  or on call removes the risk they feel from working with a newer agent.

The cognitive bias to lean into: Endowment Effect. Their overvaluation
of the home is not a problem to overcome quickly — it's an emotion to
acknowledge before any price conversation begins.

What to never say: Anything that hurries the process or minimises the
emotional weight of the decision. "It's just a property" ends the
relationship before it starts.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE CYNICAL VETERAN
─────────────────────
What they need: Proof. Verifiable data. Track record they can check.
What they fear: Being manipulated again. Falling for the high quote.

New agent proof gap: This is your hardest archetype in Year 1.
They have a trained resistance to everything that sounds like a pitch —
and "I'm new but enthusiastic" sounds exactly like a pitch to them.

What fills the gap:
→ Honesty is the only credible move. Acknowledge you don't have a
  personal track record yet — but have the agency data ready and be
  specific about what you do bring.
→ Avoid any kind of oversell. Quote realistically — below what
  competitors might quote — and explain your methodology in detail.
  For this archetype, a conservative, well-reasoned price is more
  credible than a high one from an agent they've just met.
→ Where possible, have your principal or mentor available to validate
  the appraisal. A second set of experienced credentials in the room
  reduces the credibility gap significantly.
→ If the relationship feels strained, be honest about it:
  "I sense you've had a frustrating experience with agents before.
  I'd rather earn your trust slowly than make promises I can't back
  up yet."

The cognitive bias to lean into: Consistency Heuristic. Do exactly
what you say you will do, when you say you will. Every small kept
commitment builds the credibility that a track record would otherwise
provide.

Priority note: Be selective about pursuing this archetype actively
in Year 1. They are the hardest to win without proof and the most
likely to leave a difficult first impression that spreads. Prioritise
other archetypes while you build your record, unless the opportunity
is exceptional or the referral is warm.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE STATUS SELLER
───────────────────
What they need: Premium handling. An agent who understands aspiration.
What they fear: Being undersold — financially and symbolically.

New agent proof gap: They want to see that the agent selling their home
reflects the calibre of the home itself. Experience is usually part
of that signal.

What fills the gap:
→ Presentation, preparation, and polish are your proof here. The
  quality of your appraisal documentation, the care in how you've
  researched their property and suburb, the language you use — all
  of these are signals of calibre before you have results to cite.
→ Agency brand carries weight with this archetype more than any other.
  Being part of a respected agency with a premium positioning in the
  market is a legitimate status signal even for a new agent.
→ Be specific about your marketing approach and the quality of
  presentation you'll bring to the campaign. Photography, copywriting,
  how the property will be featured and positioned. For Status Sellers,
  the campaign is the story — and a new agent who thinks about the
  story is more compelling than an experienced one who doesn't.
→ Don't apologise for being new. Own your preparation and your
  commitment to their campaign specifically.

The cognitive bias to lean into: Halo Effect. First impressions matter
enormously here. Show up with your best preparation, clearest
documentation, and most considered perspective on their property.
The halo from a strong first meeting carries further with this
archetype than any other.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE PRESSURE SELLER
─────────────────────
What they need: Speed, discretion, and someone who won't make a hard
situation harder.
What they fear: Being judged, exposed, or taken advantage of.

New agent proof gap: They need to trust someone quickly. Experience
is usually a trust accelerator — but warmth and clarity can fill
that gap effectively.

What fills the gap:
→ This is the archetype where your newness is most irrelevant. They
  don't need your track record — they need your full attention,
  your discretion, and a simple, clear plan.
→ Cognitive Ease is the dominant bias. Make everything as simple and
  clear as possible. One-page process. Plain language. No jargon.
  The complexity of real estate should be invisible to them.
→ Discretion is a specific commitment — make it explicit. "I will
  not discuss your circumstances with anyone. The campaign will be
  run professionally and privately." Most agents imply discretion.
  Stating it clearly is more powerful.
→ Speed without chaos: have a clear 14 or 21-day fast-track campaign
  plan ready to show if needed. Sellers in pressure situations are
  often relieved to see that speed is possible with a clear process.

The cognitive bias to lean into: Trust Bias. Your warmth, consistency,
and clear communication are the proof they need. No track record
required — just follow-through on every commitment, no matter how small.

Priority note: This archetype is one of the most accessible for new
agents. The bar is empathy and reliability — not history.

──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

THE ANALYTICAL CONTROLLER
───────────────────────────
What they need: Data, logic, and a process they can interrogate.
What they fear: Being talked into something that doesn't hold up.

New agent proof gap: They will research you. They will check your
listings. They may find nothing — and they will notice.

What fills the gap:
→ Get ahead of it. Acknowledge early that you don't yet have a long
  personal record — and present the agency data, your research
  methodology, and your approach to the appraisal in a way that
  shows rigour. They respect honesty that is backed by preparation.
→ Bring more data than any other agent will. Comparable sales with
  specific analysis. Market trend data. A written pricing methodology.
  The volume of your preparation signals competence even without
  personal results.
→ Invite their questions. "I'd rather you push back on my analysis
  than accept it — I'd welcome your view on the comparables." This
  disarms their scepticism and positions you as confident rather than
  defensive.
→ Your principal's data is critical here. "My analysis has been
  reviewed by [name], who has X results in this suburb" provides the
  verification layer they need.

The cognitive bias to lean into: Information Bias. Give them more
material to work with — well structured, well sourced — and their
instinct to analyse will work in your favour rather than against it.

ARCHETYPE PRIORITY GUIDE — YEAR 1
───────────────────────────────────
Which seller types to pursue and which to approach with caution
while you build your first 12 months of proof.

PRIORITY: Lead with these
  ✓ Pressure Seller — empathy and reliability close this; not history
  ✓ Anxious Upgrader — process precision fills the proof gap well
  ✓ Status Seller — preparation and polish carry significant weight
  ✓ Reluctant Downsizer — patience and genuine curiosity are your edge

APPROACH WITH CAUTION: Build proof before pursuing actively
  ⚠ Analytical Controller — manageable with thorough preparation
    and principal support, but they will research you
  ✗ Cynical Veteran — hardest archetype in Year 1; prioritise after
    you have at least 4–6 settled results to reference

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "I'm a new agent meeting a [archetype] this week. I have no personal
   sales yet but my agency has strong data in the suburb. Write me a
   full appraisal conversation guide — what to open with, how to handle
   the track record question, and how to close for the listing."

→ "The seller is going to ask how many properties I've sold. Write me
   three honest, confident ways to answer that question for a
   [archetype] — without deflecting or over-apologising."

→ "Write me a one-page leave-behind document for a new agent that
   makes the case for hiring me — built around process precision
   and agency results rather than a personal track record."

→ "I'm worried about the 'why should I choose you' question. I'm new,
   my competitor has 10 years' experience. Write me the honest,
   confident version of that answer for a [archetype] seller."

→ "Help me build my borrowed proof stack. My agency is [agency] in
   [suburb]. What data should I be gathering from my principal this
   week to use in appraisals?"

→ "Write me the opening 5 minutes of an appraisal conversation with
   a Reluctant Downsizer — as a new agent who leads with curiosity
   and process, not track record."
