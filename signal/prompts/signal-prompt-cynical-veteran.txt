SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE CYNICAL VETERAN
──────────────────────────────────────
Has sold before. Thinks all agents are fundamentally the same. Has been
overpromised and underdelivered. Only responds to proof — not charm.

DOMINANT FEAR
Being manipulated again. Falling for the same high price estimate used
as a listing tool, only to be conditioned down weeks later.

DOMINANT DESIRE
An agent who doesn't perform. Who speaks plainly, backs everything with
evidence, and does exactly what they said they would.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Confirmation Bias — They've decided agents over-promise. Every interaction
  confirms the theory. A high quote proves you're chasing the listing.
  A reasonable quote proves you're incompetent. The belief is self-sealing —
  which means the only way in is evidence that disrupts the pattern entirely.

Social Proof (inverted) — The agent with the most boards triggers suspicion,
  not confidence. Volume reads as marketing, not results. The conventional
  proof mechanisms work against you with this archetype.

Authority Scepticism — Credentials, years of experience, brand name —
  these are exactly the claims that have been used to manipulate them before.
  They've learned to filter these out. Only verifiable results count.

Consistency Heuristic — They watch what you do, not what you say. The first
  small commitment — returning a call when you said you would, sending the
  data you promised — is used as a prediction model for the entire campaign.
  Micro-reliability is the entire audition.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Evidence-only price talk — a specific number you can defend, not a range
• Acknowledging out loud that agents often over-quote to win listings
• Specific case studies with verifiable outcomes (address, price, days)
• Being the agent who gives them a lower, defensible number and explains why
• Doing exactly what you say you'll do before they've even agreed to anything

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Any enthusiasm that isn't backed by specific data
• Talking about your "passion" for real estate
• Name-dropping agencies, awards, or volume without verified outcomes
• Proposing the highest appraisal figure in the room

NEVER SAY
• "I think we could achieve significantly more than that."
• "Trust me — I know this market inside out."
• "We have a huge database of ready buyers."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype is most activated at Stage 3 (Agent Awareness) — they're
watching you before they call. What you do in the suburb, how you handle
other sellers' campaigns, the quality of your signage and materials — all
of this is being evaluated. By the appraisal they have strong priors.
Your job in Stage 4 is to disrupt their expectation of being disappointed.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Strongest match. Data-led, evidence-based, no
  performance. This is exactly what the Veteran is looking for. Come
  with more proof than they expected and you'll stand out immediately.

Calm Executor — Friction pairing. Process-talk reads as a script to this
  seller. Anchor every process claim in a specific outcome. "Here's
  exactly what I do — and here's a campaign where you can verify it."

Fierce Advocate — Strong match IF backed with specifics. Stories of
  holding firm on price will resonate — but only with exact figures
  and verifiable outcomes. Heat without substance confirms their fears.

Trusted Advisor — High friction. Relationship language triggers the
  "agents say this" filter instantly. Lead with suburb data, follow
  with relationship. Never lead with the relationship.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "Write me an appraisal opening that immediately signals I'm different
   from every agent they've seen before — without saying 'I'm different
   from every agent they've seen before.'"

→ "Another agent has quoted $200k more than me. How do I handle this
   conversation in a way that builds my credibility rather than
   undermining theirs?"

→ "Write me a pre-appraisal letter to send 48 hours before I arrive —
   something that sets a tone of evidence and plain-speaking before
   I'm even in the room."

→ "What are the exact first 3 questions I should ask to let this seller
   tell me about their bad experience — and how do I respond in a way
   that builds trust without performing empathy?"

→ "Write a post-appraisal follow-up for a Cynical Veteran that adds
   one piece of new data — and nothing else."

→ "How do I present my fee to this archetype without triggering their
   'all agents are the same' filter?"
