SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE STATUS SELLER
────────────────────────────────────
The home is an identity object. The sale is a public performance of
taste and success. Wants premium handling — and will judge you on
aesthetics as much as outcomes.

DOMINANT FEAR
Being undersold — not just financially, but symbolically. A result
that doesn't reflect the home's prestige or their judgment in owning it.

DOMINANT DESIRE
A premium experience from first contact to settlement. To be seen as
owning something remarkable — by the agent, the buyers, the market.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Social Comparison — Their price expectation isn't shaped by data. It's
  shaped by what the neighbour achieved, what a colleague mentioned at
  dinner, what properties in the same social tier have fetched. The
  comparable that matters to them is a peer, not a median.

Halo Effect — First impressions extend to every judgement they make
  about you. How you're dressed, the quality of your materials, the
  precision of your language — all of this creates an assumption of
  competence or its absence before you've said a word about the market.

Prestige Pricing Bias — A higher quote signals a more sophisticated
  understanding of value to this archetype. They're more reassured by
  $2.1M than $1.85M — not because they've validated the number, but
  because the higher figure matches their self-image.

Identity Preservation — Accepting a below-expectation result isn't
  just a financial loss. It's an identity threat. It says: the home
  wasn't as remarkable as you thought. The agent must protect the
  story, not just the price.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Premium marketing approach and materials from day one — before signing
• Demonstrating your own positioning clearly matches the property's tier
• Comparable prestige sales, not just suburb medians
• Showing genuine appreciation for the property's specific standout features
• Making them feel like the campaign will be a public statement of quality

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Generic or template marketing proposals
• Being visually or professionally under-presented at the appraisal
• Focusing on volume ("we sell a lot of homes in this area")
• Comparing the property to anything they'd consider beneath it
• Treating the listing conversation like every other listing conversation

NEVER SAY
• "It's a great property but buyers are pretty price-sensitive right now."
• "We'd market it the same way we do all our listings."
• "The house down the street only got $X — let's be realistic."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype is most activated at Stage 3 (Agent Awareness) and
Stage 4 (Appraisal Mode). They form strong opinions about agents from
a distance — from the quality of your signage, your social presence,
and how your materials look. If you haven't made a strong visual
impression before the appraisal, you're already behind.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Neutral. Your data must feel curated and premium,
  not just accurate. A printout from REA loses you the room. A custom
  suburb analysis, beautifully formatted, gains it.

Calm Executor — Friction pairing. Process doesn't excite this seller.
  Before you show them a timeline, show them what a premium campaign
  looks like. Upgrade your leave-behind materials significantly.

Fierce Advocate — Strongest match. You fight for top dollar and make
  buyers feel they might miss out. That's exactly what a Status Seller
  wants to hear — and believe. Back every claim with a specific result.

Trusted Advisor — Neutral. Community embeddedness matters, but prestige
  results matter more. Anchor in comparable premium sales from your
  network, not just years in the suburb.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "Write me an appraisal opening that immediately signals I operate
   at the level of this property — through the language I use, the
   questions I ask, and the way I engage with the home itself."

→ "The seller's price expectation is significantly above the market.
   How do I manage this down without triggering their identity
   around the home's prestige?"

→ "Write me a marketing proposal introduction paragraph for this
   property that treats it as a distinct market event — not a listing."

→ "What should my leave-behind materials look like and contain for
   this archetype — and what would immediately disqualify me if I
   brought the wrong thing?"

→ "Another agent has quoted $500k more than me. How do I address this
   without looking like the lesser option?"

→ "Write a post-appraisal follow-up for this seller that feels premium
   and considered — not a standard 'just checking in' message."
