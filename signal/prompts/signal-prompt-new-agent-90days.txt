THE TRANSITION MAP
───────────────────
Every agent's claim architecture goes through three phases.
Most never think about this deliberately — and it shows.

Phase 1 — Borrowed Authority (Months 1–3)
  Your proof is your agency's, your mentor's, and your preparation.
  Your claim is about process, attention, and access to experience.
  This is where you are now. It is a legitimate place to operate —
  not a gap to hide, but a positioning to own while you build.

Phase 2 — First Proof (Months 3–9)
  Your first settled results. Your first testimonials. Your first
  real numbers. Each is small individually. Together they form the
  beginning of a pattern you can reference.
  This is where most agents start making mistakes — rushing to
  use weak data as if it were strong data. Precision and honesty
  matter more here than volume.

Phase 3 — Owned Authority (Month 9+)
  You have enough of your own data to build a genuine result claim.
  Your claim architecture shifts from process-first to result-supported.
  The conditioning sprint becomes exponentially more powerful because
  every touchpoint can reference something verifiable.

This pack covers the specific actions that move you through all three.

MONTH 1 — BORROWED AUTHORITY
──────────────────────────────
The goal: Build your foundation proof stack and begin planting
presence in the suburb — without needing personal results to do it.

PROOF ACTIONS

Capture your agency data.
  Sit with your principal this week and extract: office median days
  on market in your suburb for the last 12 months; number of sales;
  clearance rate if auction-focused; price range distribution.
  This data is yours to use — correctly attributed to the team.

Draft your process document.
  Write a one-page campaign process document that explains exactly
  what happens between listing and settlement. Be specific enough
  that no seller could read it and say "every agent says this."
  Include: reporting frequency, open for inspection process,
  feedback mechanism, price feedback protocol, offer management.

Build your suburb knowledge file.
  Research your suburb thoroughly — not just to pass an exam, but
  to have specific, useful things to say in any appraisal.
  Comparable sales (last 6 months), active competition, buyer
  profile, recent infrastructure or demographic changes.
  You should be able to speak to any street in your suburb with
  specific knowledge, not general impressions.

Get your character reference ready.
  Identify two or three people (from any professional context)
  who can speak to your work ethic, reliability, and integrity.
  Ask them in advance. Know what they'll say.

CONDITIONING ACTIONS

Begin weekly suburb presence — even with zero listings.
  Post one piece of suburb market content per week.
  The quality of the data and the clarity of the insight is your
  proof of competence before you have sales to reference.
  "I don't have listings yet" is not a reason to wait. The agents
  who wait build nothing. The agents who start build a library.

Attend every open for inspection you can.
  Register as a buyer. Take notes on presentation, pricing,
  competition. This is research that will surface in appraisals
  as specific knowledge — which reads as experience.

MONTH 2 — FIRST PROOF ACCUMULATION
────────────────────────────────────
The goal: Capture every piece of evidence from your first
interactions — before you have settled sales — and begin
building the habit of documentation.

PROOF ACTIONS

After every appraisal — document it.
  Within 24 hours of every appraisal (whether you win or not),
  write a one-paragraph summary: seller archetype, what you
  presented, how they responded, what you'd do differently.
  This is your private training log. After 10 appraisals it
  becomes a pattern you can see and improve.

After every listing — document the process.
  Even before settlement: what happened, what you tried, what
  the buyer response was like, how feedback was managed.
  When settlement comes, you'll have everything ready for a
  proper case study — not a reconstructed memory.

Ask for feedback proactively.
  After every interaction — appraisals that don't convert,
  listings that are progressing — ask: "Is there anything I
  could have explained more clearly? Anything you weren't sure
  about?" This is intelligence and it is character-building.

FIRST TESTIMONIAL FRAMEWORK
  The first testimonial is the hardest to ask for.
  Most agents don't ask, or ask too vaguely.

  Ask at the right moment: 1–2 days after settlement, when
  the relief and satisfaction are highest.

  Make it easy: "I'd love to use your feedback to help other
  sellers understand what the process feels like. Would you be
  comfortable writing two or three sentences about your experience?
  Specifically — how the process felt to you, whether you felt kept
  informed, and how you felt about the outcome."

  Guide the content: A testimonial that says "great to deal with"
  is almost useless. A testimonial that says "always called within
  the day, explained every offer clearly, and never pressured me
  toward a decision I wasn't comfortable with" is proof of process.

CONDITIONING ACTIONS

Personalise your content as your knowledge deepens.
  Move from suburb-level market posts to street-level insights.
  "Three properties settled in [street] this month — here's what
  the results tell us about where buyer appetite is sitting."
  Specificity is proof of attention.

Begin direct outreach to warm contacts.
  A former colleague, a family connection, someone you know
  who has mentioned thinking about selling. Not a sales call —
  a genuine check-in. "I'm learning this area deeply at the
  moment. If you ever want an honest read on the market,
  I'm happy to talk with no agenda."

MONTH 3 — CLAIM FORMATION
───────────────────────────
The goal: Take what you've accumulated and form your first
real claim — even if it is small, specific, and provisional.

A provisional claim is better than no claim.
A specific small claim is better than a vague big one.

WHAT QUALIFIES AS A FIRST CLAIM

If you have even one settled result:
  "My first sale in [suburb] settled in [X days] — [Y%] above
  reserve / within [Z%] of asking price."
  One result, precisely stated, is a real claim. Don't oversell it.
  Present it as the beginning of a record, not the whole record.

If you have testimonials only:
  "Three sellers I've worked with this year described the same
  thing: they always knew where the campaign was and they never
  felt rushed toward a decision."
  A pattern across testimonials is a legitimate process claim.

If you have neither yet:
  Return to Phase 1 and be honest about where you are.
  Continue accumulating. Do not fabricate or exaggerate.
  The agents who build the most durable reputations are the ones
  who are consistently precise — not the ones who oversell early.

CLAIM FORMATION EXERCISE
  Answer these four questions in writing:

  1. What is the one thing I have done consistently in every
     seller interaction so far?

  2. What have sellers specifically commented on or thanked me for?

  3. What does my agency data support that I can legitimately use?

  4. What would I want to be known for in this suburb in three years?

  The intersection of questions 1 and 2 is your current claim.
  Questions 3 and 4 are where you're building toward.

CONCENTRATION CHECK
  Take your current claim (or the beginning of one) and check it
  against your touchpoints. Does it appear in:

  [ ] Your social media bio?
  [ ] How you introduce yourself at appraisals?
  [ ] Your follow-up messages after appraisals?
  [ ] Any printed or digital leave-behind?

  Consistency compounds. A small, true claim stated consistently
  builds faster than a big, vague claim said occasionally.

CAPTURING YOUR FIRST CASE STUDY
─────────────────────────────────
The case study is the most powerful proof asset in real estate.
One well-documented case study outperforms twenty vague references
to past experience.

A SIGNAL case study has five components:

1. SELLER SITUATION (brief, anonymised if needed)
   Who was this seller? What were they facing?
   e.g. "A couple in their 60s selling a family home they'd owned
   for 28 years. Emotionally attached, not in a hurry, wanted to
   feel heard."

2. THE CHALLENGE
   What made this sale complex, sensitive, or non-standard?
   e.g. "Timing was critical — they were purchasing before selling.
   The market had softened slightly from the previous quarter."

3. THE APPROACH
   What did you do specifically? (Process, communication, strategy)
   e.g. "We met three times before signing. I provided a written
   timeline for both the sale and their purchase coordination.
   Buyer feedback was sent within 4 hours of every open."

4. THE OUTCOME
   The result — as precisely as you can state it.
   e.g. "Sold in 18 days. Settled 6 days before their purchase,
   giving them certainty on both sides without a bridging loan."

5. SELLER VOICE (optional but powerful)
   One sentence from the seller — with their permission.
   e.g. "I always felt like I knew exactly where we were."

This case study format works for any archetype and any price range.
Your first case study, however small the sale, is the beginning
of the proof library you'll draw from for years.
