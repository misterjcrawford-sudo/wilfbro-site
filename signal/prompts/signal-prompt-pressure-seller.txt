SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE PRESSURE SELLER
──────────────────────────────────────
Divorce, deceased estate, financial stress. Needs speed and discretion
above all else. Highly vulnerable — and acutely aware of it.

DOMINANT FEAR
Being judged, exposed, or taken advantage of. Having their circumstances
used as leverage against them.

DOMINANT DESIRE
Discretion, speed, and an agent who treats them with total dignity
regardless of the circumstances.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Scarcity (time) — When a timeline is forced by external pressure
  (legal, financial, personal), time feels like the scarcest resource.
  This urgency distorts every decision — they'll accept worse terms
  just to stop the uncertainty. A skilled agent manages this rather
  than exploits it.

Trust Heuristic — This seller is too depleted to evaluate agents
  carefully. They default to a single gut question: does this person
  feel safe? Whoever creates that feeling first wins — not on merit,
  but on emotional signal.

Vulnerability Sensitivity — In a weakened position, every question
  feels like probing. Every mention of their timeline feels like
  leverage being assessed. Even innocent admin questions land as
  threats. Read the room before you speak.

Decision Fatigue — They're making dozens of decisions a day across
  multiple crisis fronts. Any agent who adds complexity or creates
  more to think about will be unconsciously rejected. Simplicity and
  clarity are your most powerful tools.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Immediately creating a safe, low-pressure environment from first contact
• Being clear about timelines without being pushy about them
• Showing specific experience with estate, separation, or financial sales
• Promising — and visibly keeping — absolute confidentiality
• Removing decisions rather than presenting options

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Any language that implies judgment of their situation
• Trying to extract information before trust is established
• Performing enthusiasm when composure is needed
• Moving to price before acknowledging the human reality
• Giving them more to think about when they're already overwhelmed

NEVER SAY
• "Don't worry, these situations are totally normal — we see them all the time."
• "What's the story here — divorce or something?"
• "If you need to move fast, I can get a cash buyer in quickly."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype often enters at Stage 4 (Appraisal Mode) without
passing through the earlier stages — the sale is circumstance-driven,
not aspiration-driven. There's no "maybe" phase. They're already in
it. Which means you have little conditioning history with them and must
build trust entirely in the first conversation.

IMPORTANT NOTE ON ETHICS
This archetype is the most vulnerable seller you'll encounter. The
SIGNAL framework is built around helping them get the best outcome
from their situation — not exploiting their urgency. If you find
yourself thinking about how their timeline benefits you, reframe
immediately: your job is to make this as good as it can be for them.
That approach also wins you the listing and the referral.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Neutral with care needed. Don't lead with data.
  Lead with calm competence and a safe presence. Evidence comes after
  trust, not before.

Calm Executor — Strongest match. Certainty and predictability are
  exactly what this seller needs. Your process removes chaos. That's
  your entire pitch.

Fierce Advocate — High friction. Your energy will feel unsafe.
  Dial to quiet confidence. Let results speak through case studies,
  not intensity.

Trusted Advisor — Strong match. Safety and discretion come naturally
  to this archetype. If you have experience with similar situations
  (estate, separation), mention it plainly and early.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "Write me an appraisal opening for this seller that immediately
   creates a feeling of safety and calm — without being so careful
   that it feels managed or clinical."

→ "How do I ask the questions I need to ask (timeline, other vendors,
   financial position) without making this seller feel interrogated?"

→ "Write a first contact message (text or email) for a referral
   introduction to a Pressure Seller — before we've spoken."

→ "The seller needs a fast result but doesn't want a low-ball price.
   How do I structure the campaign and the conversation around both
   of those needs without them conflicting?"

→ "There are multiple decision-makers (siblings, executors, lawyers).
   How do I manage the communication without creating more stress?"

→ "Write a follow-up after the first conversation that confirms I
   understood everything they told me — and nothing extra."
