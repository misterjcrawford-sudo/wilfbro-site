SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE ANALYTICAL CONTROLLER
────────────────────────────────────────────
Spreadsheet-driven. Wants data, process, and logic. Has already
researched comparable sales. Distrusts charm as a substitute
for substance.

DOMINANT FEAR
Being talked into something that doesn't hold up to scrutiny. Making
an emotion-led decision they'll later regret.

DOMINANT DESIRE
An agent who gives them enough information to reach the right
conclusion themselves — without pressure.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Information Bias — They seek more information even when additional
  data wouldn't improve the decision. The research loop becomes a
  way to feel in control rather than a genuine input to a better
  outcome. You need to help them recognise when they have enough.

Overconfidence Effect — They've spent three weeks researching the
  market and believe their model is more reliable than yours. They're
  not entirely wrong — but they're missing the nuance of 40 appraisals
  this year. Never dismiss their research. Work alongside it.

Anchoring — The first price they encountered (from their own research
  or another agent) is their anchor. Everything since is measured
  against it. Be aware of what anchor they arrived with before you
  present your number.

Analysis Paralysis — The perfect moment never arrives. Every variable
  considered reveals another variable. They can delay for months while
  appearing to be progressing. A patient but structured approach keeps
  them moving.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Coming with more data than they expected — but better organised
• A clear, written process document they can review and annotate
• Welcoming their questions without defensiveness or frustration
• Letting them feel like they reached the decision, not you
• Answering every data question with a specific number, not a range

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Vague or approximate answers to specific data questions
• Relying on personality or rapport in place of substance
• Seeming uncomfortable or defensive when challenged on your numbers
• Rushing the decision or creating artificial urgency
• Enthusiasm without evidence

NEVER SAY
• "Look, at the end of the day you just have to trust your gut."
• "I can't give you an exact figure — it depends on the buyer."
• "You're overthinking it — the market will sort it out."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype is highly active from Stage 2 (Market Curiosity) onward —
they'll have built their own price model before calling you. By Stage 4
(Appraisal Mode) they're stress-testing your knowledge against their
own research. They respect agents who can withstand scrutiny and engage
their data on equal terms. Stage 5 (Decision) can stall — not from
emotion, but from continuing to seek more information.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Strongest match. Come with more data than they
  have. Engage their research seriously. When you know more than them,
  they respect it — and stop looking for a reason to doubt you.

Calm Executor — Strong match. A written process document is exactly
  what this archetype wants. Give them something to read, review,
  and return questions on.

Fierce Advocate — High friction. Every claim needs a figure. Every
  statement needs a source. "I fight for every dollar" without the
  receipts to prove it reads as exactly the kind of empty confidence
  they're guarding against.

Trusted Advisor — Friction pairing. Relationship language without
  rigour loses them. Back community claims with decade-level data.
  "I know this suburb well" must be immediately followed by proof.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "The seller has built their own comparable sales model and believes
   the property is worth significantly more than I do. How do I engage
   their analysis seriously while guiding them to a defensible number?"

→ "Write me an appraisal opening for this seller — one that immediately
   signals I've done serious preparation and am ready to engage on
   a data level."

→ "This seller is stalling — they keep asking for more information
   before committing. How do I help them recognise they already have
   enough to make a good decision?"

→ "What data should I bring to the appraisal that they're unlikely
   to have already found themselves — and how do I present it without
   being patronising?"

→ "Write a post-appraisal follow-up that adds one new relevant data
   point and gently moves the conversation forward without pressure."

→ "How do I handle it when they challenge a specific comparable I've
   used in my price justification?"
