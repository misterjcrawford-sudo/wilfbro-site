THE CLAIM ARCHITECTURE FRAMEWORK
──────────────────────────────────
A claim is not a tagline. It is the single, specific thing you are
known for in the mind of a potential seller — before they call.

Most agents have no claim. They have a description.
"Experienced local agent with a passion for results" is not a claim.
It is category noise. Every agent says a version of it.

A claim is narrower, more specific, and more verifiable. It answers
one question: why you, rather than anyone else?

THE THREE CLAIM TYPES
─────────────────────
Process Claim — You are known for HOW you work.
  Examples:
  • "Every seller gets a written 28-day campaign schedule before
    signing. No surprises."
  • "I give one price, not a range. I can defend every figure."
  • "My vendors get a call within 24 hours of every open for inspection —
    full buyer feedback, not a summary."

  Best for: Calm Executor, Analytical Controller sellers
  Weakest when: used without case study evidence to back it

Result Claim — You are known for WHAT you achieve.
  Examples:
  • "In the last 12 months, 9 of my 11 sales in [suburb] sold above
    reserve at auction."
  • "My median days on market in [suburb] is 18 — suburb median is 34."
  • "Every property I listed in Q4 last year sold within 3% of asking price."

  Best for: Cynical Veteran, Analytical Controller, Status Seller
  Weakest when: the numbers aren't strong — don't use a result claim
    to hide a mediocre record. Sellers can verify.

Relationship Claim — You are known for WHO you are in the community.
  Examples:
  • "I've introduced more buyers to [suburb] properties than any other
    agent — I've been here since 2011."
  • "Three generations of one family have sold with me in this street."
  • "I wrote the suburb profile that [major publication] used last year."

  Best for: Trusted Advisor, Reluctant Downsizer, Pressure Seller
  Weakest when: used without specifics — "I know this suburb" without
    a decade of data behind it reads as every other agent's line.

THE CONCENTRATION SCORE
───────────────────────
Your concentration score measures how consistently your claim appears
across your touchpoints. Rate each channel from 0–2:

  0 = Claim is absent or contradicted
  1 = Claim appears but inconsistently or vaguely
  2 = Claim is specific, consistent, and evidenced

Score each channel:

  [ ] Email signature / footer
  [ ] Social media bio
  [ ] Social media content (last 10 posts)
  [ ] Website "About" page
  [ ] Appraisal leave-behind materials
  [ ] Letterbox / door-drop pieces
  [ ] Verbal introduction at appraisal
  [ ] Post-appraisal follow-up messages
  [ ] Google Business profile
  [ ] Referral request language

Maximum score: 20
Strong claim architecture: 16+
Fragmented (typical agent): 6–10
No claim present: below 6

If your score is below 14, the most valuable thing you can do this
sprint is not more content — it is rebuilding from a single, specific,
defensible claim and rewriting every channel from there.

CLAIM BY AGENT ARCHETYPE
─────────────────────────
Market Authority — Lead with a result claim, supported by process.
  Your claim should feel like it was written by someone who tracks
  their own numbers compulsively. Because you do.
  Avoid: relationship-first language. It dilutes your authority signal.

Calm Executor — Lead with a process claim, supported by result.
  Your claim should reduce seller anxiety. "Here is exactly what
  happens" — then the outcome. Predictability is your competitive edge.
  Avoid: vague assurances. Every process claim needs a specific example.

Fierce Advocate — Lead with a result claim, delivered with conviction.
  Your claim should feel like a commitment, not a statistic. "I have
  never accepted an offer I didn't believe was the seller's best
  outcome" — then the numbers to prove it.
  Avoid: process language. It flattens your energy without matching it.

Trusted Advisor — Lead with a relationship claim, grounded in data.
  Your claim should be felt before it is read. Longevity, presence,
  community. Then the result that proves it wasn't just presence.
  Avoid: leading with results — it disconnects from your natural register.

THE CLAIM AUDIT
───────────────
Before building a new claim, audit the current one — or the absence of one.

Step 1: Find your most recent version of these materials:
  social bio, website "About", appraisal leave-behind, email footer.

Step 2: Ask: What is the single thing a seller would remember
  if they read all of these and then set them down?

Step 3: If the answer is "nothing specific" or "probably nothing" —
  you have a description, not a claim. Start the build from scratch.

Step 4: Write one sentence that answers: why you, rather than anyone?
  It must contain at least one specific, verifiable element.
  It must be something a competitor could not honestly copy.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
→ "I'm a [archetype] in [suburb]. Based on the results I've shared,
   build me a single-sentence core claim — and explain why it works
   for my archetype and the sellers I'm trying to reach."

→ "Rewrite my social bio, website About section, and email footer
   so that all three carry the same claim consistently — without
   sounding copy-pasted."

→ "Score my claim architecture. Here are my current versions of
   [channels]. Give me a concentration score and identify the
   three highest-impact rewrites."

→ "Write my verbal introduction for the appraisal — the version I
   say in the first 90 seconds. It should carry my claim naturally,
   not as a pitch."

→ "My claim is [current claim]. Is this a true claim or just a
   description? If it's a description, help me sharpen it into
   something specific and defensible."

→ "Write five variations of my core claim — each calibrated to
   a different seller archetype (Analytical Controller, Status Seller,
   Reluctant Downsizer, Cynical Veteran, Pressure Seller)."
