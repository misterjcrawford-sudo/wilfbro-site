SELLER ARCHETYPE: THE ANXIOUS UPGRADER
───────────────────────────────────────
Stretched financially. Terrified of mistiming the market. Selling and
buying simultaneously — and the stress of that coordination keeps them
up at night.

DOMINANT FEAR
Selling too cheap, buying too high, and ending up worse off than before.

DOMINANT DESIRE
Certainty. A clear plan, a timeline they can trust, and someone who
removes the chaos.

ACTIVE COGNITIVE BIASES
Loss Aversion — They feel the pain of a potential loss twice as intensely
  as the pleasure of a gain. The downside scenario dominates their thinking
  even when the numbers favour the move.

Recency Bias — Recent market movements feel permanent. If prices have
  risen, they assume they'll keep rising. If prices softened last month,
  they believe the market is collapsing.

Anchoring — The first price figure they hear lodges in their mind.
  Every subsequent estimate is measured against that anchor, regardless
  of whether it was accurate.

Worst-Case Thinking — Their mind gravitates to the worst possible outcome
  (sell low, buy high, end up worse off) even when probability is low.
  The fear feels more real than the likely outcome.

WHAT WINS THEM
• Showing you have coordinated buy-sell transitions before — with outcomes
• Giving them a step-by-step process they can see end-to-end before signing
• Specific suburb data that removes uncertainty from both sides of the move
• Testimonials or referrals from past upgraders you've guided successfully
• Being the calm, structured presence in a situation that feels chaotic

WHAT LOSES THEM
• Vague optimism ("it's a great time to sell — the market's strong")
• Not acknowledging the complexity of buying simultaneously
• Rushing the conversation or skipping logistics
• High price estimates with no basis — they'll be conditioned down later
• Any language that makes the process sound unpredictable

NEVER SAY
• "Don't worry about the timing — it always works out."
• "The market's moving fast, you need to act now."
• "We'll figure out the buy side once this one sells."

DECISION WINDOW INTENSITY
This archetype stays relatively calm in Stage 1 (Private Consideration)
but escalates sharply from Stage 2 onward. They're emotionally activated
during market research and at peak anxiety by the appraisal stage. The
decision tends to feel overwhelming — they want it made for them, but
won't admit it. Whoever gives them the clearest plan wins.

AGENT × SELLER MATCH NOTES
───────────────────────────
Market Authority — Strong match. Lead with buy-sell coordination data.
  Show comparable upgrader scenarios. Evidence over reassurance.

Calm Executor — Strongest match. This seller's primary need is certainty.
  Your process document is your listing tool — show them the full
  timeline before you discuss price.

Fierce Advocate — Friction pairing. Dial back the competitive energy.
  This seller needs composure, not heat. Advocacy language triggers
  their worst-case thinking.

Trusted Advisor — Strong match. Referrals from past upgraders are the
  highest-value proof you can offer. Lean into shared experience stories.

SUGGESTED PROMPTS
─────────────────
Use one of these after pasting — or write your own.

→ "Write me the opening 3 minutes of my appraisal with this seller.
   Start from the moment I walk in. I want to immediately signal that
   I understand their situation without them having to explain it."

→ "What are the three things most likely to lose me this listing,
   and what's the specific move to avoid each one?"

→ "The seller has been quoted $150k more by another agent. Walk me
   through how to handle that conversation without undermining the
   other agent or caving on my number."

→ "Write a post-appraisal follow-up message for this seller. It should
   add value and move them toward a decision without any pressure."

→ "What questions should I ask in the first 10 minutes to understand
   exactly where their anxiety is sitting — and what they most need
   to hear from me?"

→ "Create a one-page process overview I can leave with this seller
   after the appraisal — covering what happens from sign to settlement."
